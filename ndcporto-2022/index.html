According to quantum mechanics, information about quantum systems is available only via observables.

Observables are matrices.

For two-state systems an observable has two vectors that form

measurement basis

These are the so-called eigenvectors or eigenstates.

$\mathbf{Z}$ basis

$$ 0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \cr 0 \end{bmatrix} = \ket{0}$$ $$ 1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \cr 1 \end{bmatrix} = \ket{1} $$

$\mathbf{X}$ basis

$$ 0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \ket{+}$$ $$ 1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \ket{-} $$

In quantum computing, basis vectors map to classical bits.

These eigenvector states the only states that can be

observed

in the measurement process.

We cannot ask a quantum system

what state are you in?

For $\mathbf{Z}$ basis we can only ask

Are you $\ket{0}$ or are you $\ket{1}$?

For $\mathbf{X}$ basis we can only ask

Are you $\ket{+}$ or are you $\ket{-}$?

A quantum system can be in one of the infinitely many states possible as a linear combination of two basis states:

$$\ket{\psi} = \alpha\ket{0} + \beta\ket{1}$$

If both $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are non-zero, the system is in a

superposition

As soon as we looked at the photon - it assumed the basis state. Before that it was everywhere, and nowhere.

Upon measurement, quantum state collapses to one of the observable states

outcome is probabilistic

It is a fundamental quantum doctrine that a measurement does not reveal a pre-existing value of the measured property. On the contrary, the outcome of a measurement is brought into being by the act of measurement itself. - David Mermin

According to Born rule, there is a 50% probability of observing a collapse to $\ket{0}$ or to $\ket{1}$.

Born rule is not derived, it is an axiom in quantum mechanics, confirmed experimentally, or rather never violated by any experiment.

$$H \times H = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{bmatrix} \times \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = I $$

H is its own inverse, so applying it the second time will undo the earlier effect.

We witnessed a uniquely quantum phenomenon

uncertainty principle

In quantum mechanics we cannot simultaneously know values of

conjugate variables

with arbitrary precision.

Those are for example position & momentum or energy & time.

$\Delta x \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$

$\Delta E \Delta t \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$

By Ahmed Neutron - Own work, CC BY-SA 4.0, https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=69715938

In quantum computing, the measurements in the $\mathbf{Z}$ and $\mathbf{X}$ bases do not commute.

$X \cdot Z - Z \cdot X \neq 0$ which means the order of measurements matters!

Measurement of the spin value in the $\mathbf{Z}$ basis creates

maximum uncertainty

of the spin value in $\mathbf{X}$ the basis, and vice versa.

For sequential measurements e.g. Z-X-Z, the previous results in Z get destroyed by X measurement!

$\mathbf{X}$ basis

A state |+> is a definite state in basis X, and maps to a classical 0 with 100% probability. However, in Z basis, it has a 50% probability of collapsing to |0> or |1>.

Superposition is basis dependent.

Because of this, the choice of basis allows us to encode information in a qubit in a specific way. The information can only be accurately read, if you know the basis you should use upon measurement, otherwise the result will be random.

This concept underpins quantum cryptography.

Because arbitrary quantum states cannot be cloned, an attacker, 🦹‍♀️ Eve, would need to measure the qubits to steal information.

She must guess between $\mathbf{Z}$ and $\mathbf{X}$ bases.

👧🏻 👦🏼 Alice and Bob should compare half of their resulting bits to perform an eavesdropper check.

If 👧🏻 👦🏼 Alice and Bob find mismatches between the bits for which their bases agreed, they detected an attacker.

Otherwise the remaining half of the bits make up the shared key.

Entanglement

                
// create a pair of particles in state |up>, |up>
use pair = Qubit[2];

// measure in PauliZ or PauliX basis
let result1 = Measure([basis], [pair[0]]);
let result2 = Measure([basis], [pair[1]]);

// classically, the measurement will agree only in the Z basis
// in the X basis the results will be random
let agreement = result1 == result2;
                
            

                
// create a pair of particles in state |up>, |up>
use pair = Qubit[2];

// entangle them
PrepareEntangledState([pair[0]], [pair[1]]);

// measure in PauliZ or PauliX basis
let result1 = Measure([basis], [pair[0]]);
let result2 = Measure([basis], [pair[1]]);

// for an entangled pair, the measurement will always agree
let agreement = result1 == result2;

We witnessed a uniquely quantum phenomenon

entanglement

We dealt here with a maximally entangled state.

$$\ket{\psi} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\ket{00} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\ket{11}$$

Upon measurement, there is a 50% probability of collapsing to $\ket{00}$ or $\ket{11}$.

Superposition is "contagious", it spills into the overall system state until it collapses via the act of measurement. The results are truly random.

To describe the state of a multi-qubit system, we use a tensor product of the individual qubit states.

$$\ket{\psi_1} = \alpha_1\ket{0} + \beta_1\ket{1} \;\;\; \ket{\psi_2} = \alpha_2\ket{0} + \beta_2\ket{1}$$

$$\ket{\psi_{12}} = \alpha_1\alpha_2\ket{00} + \alpha_1\beta_2\ket{01} + \beta_1\alpha_2\ket{10} + \beta_1\beta_2\ket{11}$$

This works well for independent qubits but breaks down for entangled states.

We cannot find $\alpha_1$, $\alpha_2$, $\beta_1$ and $\beta_2$ to satisfy

$\alpha_1\alpha_2\ket{00} + \alpha_1\beta_2\ket{01} + \beta_1\alpha_2\ket{10} + \beta_1\beta_2\ket{11} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} + \ket{11})$

We cannot decompose an entangled quantum state into a tensor product of the individual quantum states.

This tells us, at the quantum level, the smallest possible unit of reality is the composite system consisting of those two entangled objects. They cannot be treated as independent anymore. The problem is they can physically be separate from each other.

A single quantum state can span across multiple spatially separated quantum objects, which seem to affect each other instantly.

Einstein called it

"spooky action at a distance"

There is a theorem + an experiment called Bell's test (Bell's inequalities) that prove that this cannot be explained with classical local physics.

We call this

non-locality
