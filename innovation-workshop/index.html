Quantum Computing

The strength of quantum computers is that they are able to take algorithmic advantage of superposition. This is called

quantum parallelism

  1. Create a superposition of all possible results
  2. Use interference to amplify correct result, and cancel out bad results
...quantum parallelism allows quantum computers to evaluate a function $f(x)$ for many different values of x simultaneously.

- Nielsen and Chuang

For example, instead of evaluating a function many times with all possible inputs, we can evaluate once with superposition of inputs.

Deutsch's problem - given an unknown function

$f : \{0,1\} \rightarrow \{0,1\}$

determine if the function is constant or balanced

  • $f_0$ is constant
  • $f_1$ is constant
  • $f_2$ is balanced
  • $f_3$ is balanced
  • $f_0(0) = f_0(1) = 0$
  • $f_1(0) = f_1(1) = 1$
  • $f_2(0) = 0 \;\;\; f_2(1) = 1$
  • $f_3(0) = 1 \;\;\; f_3(1) = 0$

Classically, two function evaluations are needed.

After the first execution, we still cannot say if the function is balanced or constant.

On a quantum computer, thanks to interference, the answer can be obtained in just one call.

If there is one thing quantum mechanics can teach us, it is to

abandon our intuition

What we perceive as intuition is not a mystic sixth sense or a magical foresight, but rather a complex, yet, streamlined processing of our experiences, biases, learned knowledge, subconscious observations, and emotional context. It shapes our cognition, our decision-making process, and ultimately our understanding of the world but can hinder innovation.

Modern quantum mechanics was born in 1925 - with the breakthrough paper by Werner Heisenberg.

Heisenberg abandonded trying to describe electrons in the atom using classical orbits and classical equations of motion, and introduced a completely abstract, matrix-like approach - the concept of observables (restricted what we can know).

Wolfgang Pauli, Werner Heisenberg, Paul Dirac and Enrico Fermi were all in their mid-20s when they laid the robust conceptual foundations of quantum mechanics.

It became known as Knabenphysik.
