Quantum Mechanics

Quantum physics dates back to 1900, when Max Planck introduced the concept of quanta - a discrete packet of energy.

Planck scale is unimaginabily small.

$h = 6.62607015 \times 10^{-34}\,\mathrm{J\cdot s}$

Quantum mechanics was born exactly 100 years ago - with the breakthrough paper by Werner Heisenberg.

It is the foundation of all quantum physics and allows for probabilistic prediction of atomic and subatomic phenonomena.

Heisenberg introduced some key foundational ideas in the paper:
  • the concept of observables (restricted what we can know). He focused on "relationships between quantities that in principle are observable"
  • the fact that observables do not always commute, namely P*Q != Q*P
  • he used was a form of matrices (matrix mechanics) later reformulated as mathematically equivalent wave mechanics. this nicely ties into QC

Quantum mechanics is one of the most successful scientific theories of all time.

It has enabled uprecedented progress - without it, there would be no modern chemistry, technology or medicine.

  • transistors
  • lasers, supeconductors, semiconductors
  • GPS
  • material science, chemistry
  • MRI

Mach-Zehnder Interferometer

operation MachZehnder() : Result {
    // allocate a new qubit in default state
    use photon = Qubit();

    // beam splitter
    H(photon);

    // second beam splitter
    H(photon);

    // result is a certain Zero ("detector2") due to interference
    MResetZ(photon)
}

Mach-Zehnder Interferometer with QNDs

The photon behaves differently depending on

whether we look or not

operation MachZehnderWithQND() : (Result, Result) {
    // allocate a new qubit in default state
    use photon = Qubit();

    // beam splitter
    H(photon);

    // observe using the QND - it is a random Zero or One ("qnd1" or "qnd2")
    let qndResult = MResetZ(photon);

    // second beam splitter
    H(photon);

    // result is now a random Zero or One (interference disappears)
    let detectorResult = MResetZ(photon);

    (qndResult, detectorResult)
}

We witnessed a uniquely quantum phenomenon

superposition

A qubit state is described by a two-dimensional vector.

$\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \cr \beta \end{bmatrix}$

An observable defines pairs of states that can be observed.

$$ \sigma_z=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \sigma_x=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \sigma_y=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

These are:

$$ \sigma_z \rightarrow \ket{0} \; \ket{1} \qquad \sigma_x \rightarrow \ket{+} \; \ket{-} \qquad \sigma_y \rightarrow \ket{+i} \; \ket{-i} $$

Observables are matrices.

We cannot ask a quantum system

what state are you in?

For $\mathbf{Z}$ basis measurement, we can only ask

Are you $\ket{0}$ or are you $\ket{1}$?

For $\mathbf{Z}$ basis measurement, if a system is neither $\ket{0}$, nor $\ket{1}$ state, it is in a superposition.

Mathematically, superposition is a linear combination of the observable states.

As soon as we looked at the photon - it assumed the basis state. Notice if $\alpha = 1$ and $\beta = 0$ we are in basis state $\ket{0}$ and vice versa.

Upon measurement, superposition state collapses to one of the observable states

outcome is probabilistic

It is a fundamental quantum doctrine that a measurement does not reveal a pre-existing value of the measured property. On the contrary, the outcome of a measurement is brought into being by the act of measurement itself. - David Mermin
The world is not as real as we think

- Anton Zeilinger

Schrödinger's cat was both dead and alive at the same time. It's NOT that he was dead or alive and Schrödinger simply did not know. Only once Schrödinger opened the box, because of "observation", would the cat's fate be decided.

Until observed, the cat seems to be in the superposition of dead and alive.

The solution to the paradox is decoherence.

$\sigma_x \rightarrow \ket{+} \; \ket{-}$

                operation SpinZ(measurement_basis : Pauli) : Result {

We witnessed a uniquely quantum phenomenon

uncertainty principle

In quantum mechanics we cannot simultaneously know values of

conjugate variables

with arbitrary precision.

The measurements in the $\mathbf{Z}$ and $\mathbf{X}$ bases do not commute. Measurement in the $\mathbf{Z}$ basis creates

maximum uncertainty

for the measurement in the $\mathbf{X}$ the basis, and vice versa.

For sequential measurements e.g. Z-X-Z, the previous results in Z get destroyed by X measurement!

Entanglement

Entangled pair, measured in the $\mathbf{Z}$ basis.

                operation Entangled(measurement_basis : Pauli) : Result[] {
    use (qubit1, qubit2) = (Qubit(), Qubit());

    // create entangled state
    H(qubit1);
    CNOT(qubit1, qubit2);

    // measure in any basis, as long as it is the same for both qubits
    let result1 = Measure([measurement_basis], [qubit1]);
    let result2 = Measure([measurement_basis], [qubit2]);
    ResetAll([qubit1, qubit2]);

    // the measurement results will be perfectly correlated
    [result1, result2]
}

We witnessed a uniquely quantum phenomenon

entanglement

There are two possible explanations here

🤝 the electrons carried hidden instructions from the start — pre-agreement about how to respond to measurements

⚡ the electrons are entangled, and their measurement outcomes are instantly correlated without any classical signal traveling between them

Spooky action at a distance

- Albert Einstein

There is a theorem + an experiment called Bell's test (Bell's inequalities) that prove that this cannot be explained with classical local physics.

Bell's theorem

  • 📐 If electrons carried fixed outcomes, correlation $C$ would fall linearly, so at 45° we get 50% correlation
  • ⚛️ Quantum mechanics predicts instead that $C=cos(\theta)$ - so at 45° we get ~70% correlation

To describe the state of a multi-qubit system, we use a tensor product of the individual states.

$$ \begin{bmatrix}a_1 \\ b_1 \end{bmatrix} \otimes \begin{bmatrix}a_2 \\ b_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}a_1a_2 \\ a_1b_2 \\ b_1a_2 \\ b_1b_2 \end{bmatrix} $$

However, entangled states

cannot be decomposed

into a tensor product of the individual quantum states.

This tells us, at the quantum level, the smallest possible unit of reality is the composite system consisting of those two entangled objects. They cannot be treated as independent anymore. The problem is they can physically be separate from each other.

A single quantum state can span across multiple spatially separated quantum objects, which seem to affect each other instantly.

We call this

non-locality
